# Chemistry

## Bohr-Rutherford Diagrams and Ions

- The atomic number gives the number of protons.
- A neutral atom has the same number of protons and electrons.
- The mass number is the total number of protons and neutrons.
- Metals usually lose valence electrons and form positive ions.
- Non-metals usually gain valence electrons and form negative ions.
- Example: a magnesium atom has 12 protons and 12 electrons arranged as `2-8-2`. It loses two valence electrons to form `Mg2+`, which has 10 electrons arranged as `2-8`.

## Common Ionic Charges

| Periodic table group | Common charge |
| --- | ---: |
| Group 1 | `+1` |
| Group 2 | `+2` |
| Group 13 | `+3` |
| Group 14 | `+4` or `-4` |
| Group 15 | `-3` |
| Group 16 | `-2` |
| Group 17 | `-1` |
| Group 18 | `0` |

## Diatomic Elements

The seven elements that naturally occur as two-atom molecules are:

`H2`, `N2`, `O2`, `F2`, `Cl2`, `Br2`, and `I2`.

Example:

`2Na(s) + Cl2(g) -> 2NaCl(s)`

## Conservation of Mass and Balancing

- Matter is not created or destroyed during a chemical reaction.
- The total mass of the reactants equals the total mass of the products.
- Example: if 25 g of one reactant combines with 75 g of another, the products have a total mass of 100 g.
- Balance an equation by changing coefficients, never subscripts.
- Use the lowest possible whole-number coefficients.

## Types of Chemical Reactions

| Reaction type | General pattern | Description |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Synthesis | `A + B -> AB` | Two or more substances form one product. |
| Decomposition | `AB -> A + B` | One compound breaks into simpler substances. |
| Combustion | `hydrocarbon + O2 -> CO2 + H2O` | A hydrocarbon reacts with oxygen. |
| Neutralization | `acid + base -> water + salt` | An acid reacts with a base. |
| Single displacement | `A + BC -> AC + B` | One element replaces another in a compound. |
| Double displacement | `AB + CD -> AD + CB` | Ions in two compounds exchange partners. |

## Naming Compounds and Writing Formulas

### Ionic compounds

- Ionic compounds usually contain a metal and a non-metal.
- Write the metal name first, then change the non-metal ending to `-ide`.
- Use ion charges to make the compound electrically neutral.
- Reduce subscripts to the lowest whole-number ratio.
- If the metal is multivalent, use a Roman numeral to show its charge.

Examples:

- `Co2(SO4)3` is **cobalt(III) sulfate**.
- `FePO4` is **iron(III) phosphate**.

### Molecular compounds

- Molecular compounds contain only non-metals.
- Use prefixes such as `mono-`, `di-`, `tri-`, `tetra-`, `penta-`, and `hexa-` to show the number of atoms.
- Do not reduce the subscripts of a molecular formula.

Examples:

- `N2O4` is **dinitrogen tetroxide**.
- `C3H8` contains three carbon atoms and eight hydrogen atoms.

# Biology

## Levels of Organization

`cell -> tissue -> organ -> organ system -> organism`

## Plant and Animal Cells

Both plant and animal cells contain a cell membrane, cytoplasm, nucleus, mitochondria, ribosomes, and Golgi apparatus.

| Plant cells | Animal cells |
| --- | --- |
| Cell wall | No cell wall |
| Chloroplasts | No chloroplasts |
| One large central vacuole | Smaller vacuoles |

## Cell Cycle and Mitosis

### Interphase

- `G1`: the cell grows and performs its regular functions.
- `S`: DNA is copied.
- `G2`: the cell grows and prepares to divide.

### Mitosis: PMAT

| Stage | Main event |
| --- | --- |
| Prophase | Chromosomes condense, the nuclear membrane disappears, and spindle fibres form. |
| Metaphase | Chromosomes line up across the middle of the cell. |
| Anaphase | Spindle fibres pull sister chromatids to opposite sides. |
| Telophase | New nuclear membranes form around each set of chromosomes. |

**Cytokinesis** divides the cytoplasm and produces two daughter cells.

## Cancer and Stem Cells

- Cancer occurs when cells divide uncontrollably.
- A **benign** tumour remains localized and does not invade nearby tissues.
- A **malignant** tumour invades nearby tissue and may spread to other parts of the body.
- **Totipotent** stem cells can become every cell type, including supporting embryonic tissues.
- **Pluripotent** stem cells can become almost any body cell.

## Plant Tissues and Transport

| Tissue or structure | Function |
| --- | --- |
| Meristematic tissue | Produces new cells for plant growth. |
| Dermal tissue | Protects the outside of the plant. |
| Ground tissue | Performs photosynthesis, storage, and support. |
| Vascular tissue | Transports materials through the plant. |
| Xylem | Carries water and minerals mainly upward from the roots. |
| Phloem | Carries sugars and other food through the plant. |
| Root hairs | Increase surface area for water and mineral absorption. |
| Cuticle | Reduces water loss from leaves. |
| Guard cells and stomata | Control gas exchange and water loss. |
| Palisade mesophyll | Main location of photosynthesis. |
| Spongy mesophyll | Contains air spaces that support gas exchange. |
| Flowers | Carry out sexual reproduction. |

Water rises through a plant because of root pressure, cohesion between water molecules, adhesion to xylem walls, and transpiration from the leaves.

## Animal Tissues

| Tissue type | Main function and examples |
| --- | --- |
| Epithelial | Covers surfaces, lines organs, and forms protective barriers. |
| Connective | Supports or connects structures; includes blood, adipose tissue, cartilage, and bone. |
| Muscle | Produces movement; includes skeletal, smooth, and cardiac muscle. |
| Nervous | Receives sensory information and sends electrical signals. |

## Digestive System

- The mouth begins mechanical digestion, and salivary amylase begins breaking down starch.
- The stomach churns food. Hydrochloric acid creates an acidic environment, and pepsin digests protein.
- Sphincters control the movement of food into and out of the stomach.
- In the duodenum, sodium bicarbonate from the pancreas neutralizes stomach acid.
- Pancreatic amylase digests carbohydrates, lipase digests fats, and trypsin digests proteins.
- The liver makes bile, the gallbladder stores it, and bile emulsifies fats.
- The jejunum and ileum absorb nutrients through villi and microvilli.
- The large intestine absorbs water, minerals, and some vitamins.
- The rectum stores feces, and the anus removes them from the body.

## Circulatory System

| Blood component | Function |
| --- | --- |
| Red blood cells | Carry oxygen and some carbon dioxide. |
| White blood cells | Defend the body against pathogens. |
| Platelets | Help blood clot and begin tissue repair. |

- Arteries carry blood away from the heart.
- Veins carry blood toward the heart.
- Capillaries are thin vessels where gases, nutrients, and wastes are exchanged.

### Path of blood

`body -> vena cava -> right atrium -> right ventricle -> pulmonary artery -> lungs -> pulmonary vein -> left atrium -> left ventricle -> aorta -> body`

## Respiratory System

Air follows this pathway:

`nose or mouth -> pharynx -> larynx -> trachea -> bronchi -> bronchioles -> alveoli`

- Mucus traps particles, and cilia move the mucus out of the airways.
- Gas exchange occurs in the alveoli.
- During inhalation, the diaphragm contracts and moves downward.
- During exhalation, the diaphragm relaxes and moves upward.

## Excretory System and Medical Imaging

- The kidneys filter blood, regulate water and dissolved substances, and produce urine.
- Common medical imaging methods include X-rays, MRI, ultrasound, CT scans, and endoscopy.

# Optics

## Nature of Light and the Electromagnetic Spectrum

- Light behaves as both a particle, called a **photon**, and an electromagnetic wave.
- From longest wavelength to shortest wavelength, the electromagnetic spectrum is:

`radio -> microwave -> infrared -> visible -> ultraviolet -> X-ray -> gamma ray`

- Visible colours from longest wavelength to shortest wavelength are:

`red -> orange -> yellow -> green -> blue -> indigo -> violet`

## Sources of Light

| Type | How light is produced | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Incandescence | A hot object emits light and heat. | Candle, Sun, campfire |
| Fluorescence | Ultraviolet energy is absorbed and quickly released as visible light. | Fluorescent lamp |
| Phosphorescence | Stored energy is released slowly after the original source is removed. | Glow-in-the-dark material |
| Bioluminescence | A chemical reaction produces light in a living organism. | Firefly, anglerfish |
| Chemiluminescence | A chemical reaction produces light with little heat. | Glow stick |

## Reflection

The law of reflection states:

`angle of incidence = angle of reflection`

Both angles are measured from the normal.

### Plane mirrors

A plane mirror forms an image that is:

- the same size as the object;
- upright;
- virtual;
- laterally inverted;
- the same distance behind the mirror as the object is in front.

### Curved mirrors

| Mirror and object position | Image characteristics |
| --- | --- |
| Convex mirror | Smaller, upright, virtual, and behind the mirror. |
| Concave mirror: object beyond `C` | Smaller, inverted, real, and between `C` and `F`. |
| Concave mirror: object between `C` and `F` | Larger, inverted, real, and beyond `C`. |
| Concave mirror: object between `F` and the mirror | Larger, upright, virtual, and behind the mirror. |

For a curved mirror ray diagram:

1. Draw one ray parallel to the principal axis; it reflects through `F`.
2. Draw one ray through `F`; it reflects parallel to the principal axis.
3. A ray through `C` reflects back along the same path.

## Refraction

- Refraction is the bending of light when it changes speed while entering a different medium.
- From a less optically dense medium to a more optically dense medium, light bends **toward the normal**.
- From a more optically dense medium to a less optically dense medium, light bends **away from the normal**.
- At many boundaries, some light is reflected and the rest is refracted.

### Critical angle and total internal reflection

- The critical angle occurs when the refracted ray travels along the boundary at 90 degrees to the normal.
- Total internal reflection occurs when light travels from a more optically dense medium to a less optically dense medium and the incident angle is greater than the critical angle.

## Optical Phenomena

- **Shimmering** and **mirages** occur when light refracts through layers of air at different temperatures.
- A **rainbow** forms through refraction, internal reflection, and dispersion in water droplets.
- A **sundog** is caused by dispersion and refraction through ice crystals.
- **Apparent depth** makes an underwater object appear closer to the surface than it really is.

## Lenses

| Lens and object position | Image characteristics |
| --- | --- |
| Diverging lens | Smaller, upright, virtual, and on the object's side of the lens. |
| Converging lens: object between lens and `F` | Larger, upright, and virtual. |
| Converging lens: object beyond `F` | Inverted and real; its size depends on the object's position. |

### SALT image description

- `S`: size
- `A`: attitude, upright or inverted
- `L`: location
- `T`: type, real or virtual

## The Eye and Vision Correction

Important structures include the cornea, iris, lens, retina, and ciliary muscles.

- **Myopia**: light focuses before the retina. It is corrected with a diverging lens.
- **Hyperopia**: light would focus behind the retina. It is corrected with a converging lens.

## Optics Equations

Thin lens or mirror equation:

`1/f = 1/di + 1/do`

Magnification equation:

`hi/ho = -di/do`

where `f` is focal length, `di` is image distance, `do` is object distance, `hi` is image height, and `ho` is object height.

# Climate Dynamics

## Factors That Affect Climate

### Latitude

- Latitude is the distance north or south of the equator.
- Near the equator, the Sun's rays are more concentrated.
- Near the poles, the same energy is spread over a larger area.

### Altitude

- Air temperature generally decreases as altitude increases.
- Higher locations are usually cooler than nearby lowlands.

### Bodies of water

- Water has a high heat capacity, so it heats and cools more slowly than land.
- Large bodies of water moderate temperatures in nearby areas.

### Air and ocean currents

- Winds distribute heated air through atmospheric circulation.
- Ocean currents transfer thermal energy around the planet.

### Landforms

- Mountains force air upward, causing it to cool and often produce precipitation.
- Mountain ranges can interrupt air currents and create a **rain shadow**.
- British Columbia and Alberta are examples of regions strongly affected by mountain barriers.

## Reading a Climatograph

- The line shows average monthly temperature.
- The bars show monthly precipitation.
- Temperature range = highest monthly temperature - lowest monthly temperature.
- Total annual precipitation is found by adding all monthly precipitation values.
- Use both temperature and precipitation patterns to identify the likely biome.

## Greenhouse Effect

- Earth absorbs incoming solar radiation and re-radiates part of the energy as infrared heat.
- Greenhouse gases absorb and re-emit some infrared radiation, keeping Earth warm enough for life.
- The **natural greenhouse effect** is necessary for life.
- The **anthropogenic greenhouse effect** is the additional warming caused by human activities that add more greenhouse gases to the atmosphere.

## Major Greenhouse Gases

| Gas | Important sources |
| --- | --- |
| CFCs | Human-made refrigerants and coolants. |
| Carbon dioxide, `CO2` | Fossil-fuel combustion, cement production, and cellular respiration. |
| Ozone, `O3` | Naturally present in the atmosphere; also produced by reactions involving air pollutants. |
| Nitrous oxide, `N2O` | Agriculture and fertilizers. |
| Methane, `CH4` | Permafrost melt, agriculture, livestock, decomposition, and fossil-fuel production. |
| Water vapour, `H2O` | Naturally present in the atmosphere and increases through evaporation. |

## Carbon Cycle and Carbon Sinks

- Trees, soils, and oceans act as carbon sinks by removing and storing carbon from the atmosphere.
- Burning fossil fuels moves carbon from long-term storage into the atmosphere.
- Deforestation both releases stored carbon and removes trees that would otherwise absorb `CO2`.

## Paleoclimatology and Proxy Records

**Paleoclimatology** is the study of Earth's ancient climates and temperature changes.

Scientists use proxy records because direct instrument measurements do not exist for most of Earth's history:

- **Ice cores** preserve ancient air bubbles and atmospheric information.
- **Tree rings** record yearly growing conditions.
- **Coral reefs** preserve information about ocean conditions.
- **Fossil pollen** shows which plants grew in an area.
- **Plant and animal fossils** reveal past environments.
- **Caves and sediments** preserve evidence of precipitation and other climate conditions.

## Albedo and Feedback Loops

**Albedo** describes how well a surface reflects incoming radiation.

- Snow, ice, and clouds have high albedo and reflect more energy.
- Dark soil, asphalt, and dark rock have low albedo and absorb more energy.

### Positive ice-albedo feedback

`global temperature rises -> snow and ice melt -> albedo decreases -> more solar energy is absorbed -> temperature rises further`

A positive feedback loop strengthens the original change.

## Climate Change Impacts

Possible personal and environmental impacts include:

- shorter snow seasons and less reliable winter conditions;
- changes to recreation and transportation;
- increased stress on forests and other ecosystems;
- shifts in precipitation and water availability;
- more frequent or intense heat events.
